# Data Mesh Architecture Design

## Date
2026-04-17

## Author
Steff Horemans 

## Context
In the previous ADR, we decided that we would develop our platform on Databricks and use a data mesh to build in organizational scalability from the beginning at the price of needing strict governance tools. However, several decisions need to be made about the design of the mesh to make it work for an organization:
- A data mesh is a broad concept and can very from very centralized Hub/Spoke models to fully decentralized Harmonized models. https://www.databricks.com/blog/building-data-mesh-based-databricks-lakehouse-part-2
- The core principles of a data mesh require concrete decisions:
    - Domain ownership: Data meshes push data ownership to domain owners. Hence the architecture needs to reflect this responsibility https://martinfowler.com/articles/data-mesh-principles.html
    - Data product thinking requires us to define what apart from the data itself constitutes a data product. What metadata do we need to keep, how do we guarantee availability, lineage, ... https://martinfowler.com/articles/data-mesh-principles.html
    - Self service infrastructure is needed to enable discoverability of the different domains, but requires us to define what resources are considered shared and what resources are developed and offered in the domains themselves. Without common infrastructure, different domains might use entirely different tech stacks. https://martinfowler.com/articles/data-mesh-principles.html
    - Federated governance is crucial for the pharma sector and comprises everything that is needed to make sure everyone complies with organizational and legal guidelines. 

Databricks has several resources about data meshes and various tradeoffs here: https://www.databricks.com/blog/databricks-lakehouse-and-data-mesh-part-1

## Decision
- Since we assume that the departments of Braincare are not technically mature and need to focus on their core business, we opt for a Hub/Spoke model that gives importance to the central Hub team. In this model, a central Hub team owns the shared platform infrastructure — Unity Catalog configuration, CI/CD templates, data contract standards, and compliance tooling — and acts as an internal service provider to the domain teams (spokes). Domain teams own their data products and pipelines but build them using Hub-provided patterns. Architectural decisions that affect all domains (e.g. naming conventions, access control policies, medallion layer standards) are made centrally; decisions scoped to a single domain (e.g. which source systems to ingest, domain-specific transformations) are made by the domain owner.
- **Domain ownership**: Each domain (Discovery, Translational Science, Translational Medicine, Clinical Development and Regulatory Affairs and RWE) is assigned its own Unity Catalog. The catalog boundary defines the ownership boundary — the domain data product owner controls what is published, who has access, and what SLAs apply within their catalog.

- **Data product thinking**: A data product in this platform consists of: a Delta table or set of tables at silver or gold layer, a declared schema and owner in Unity Catalog, a data contract (input/output schema + SLA + change notification policy), and lineage metadata captured automatically via Unity Catalog. The Hub team provides a template; domains are responsible for populating it.

- **Self-service infrastructure**: The Hub team owns the shared infrastructure layer: Unity Catalog, the CI/CD pipeline templates, the Databricks Declarative Automation bundle definitions, and the data contract schema. Domains provision their own compute (clusters/warehouses) within Hub-defined policies and use Hub templates to create pipelines. Domains may not introduce compute or storage outside the Databricks platform without Hub approval. Since we are deploying this demo on Databricks Free edition, everyone will need to use serverless for now.

- **Federated governance**: The Hub team defines and enforces platform-wide standards (naming conventions, tagging requirements, access control policies, medallion layer structure). Domain owners are responsible for compliance within their catalog. Enforcement is automated where possible — non-compliant data products will fail CI/CD checks. The Hub roadmap is set annually by domain representatives.

- Within each domain catalog, schemas follow a medallion pattern: bronze for raw ingested data, silver for cleaned and validated data, and gold for domain-specific aggregated or modelled data products. Cross-domain consumers are expected to read only from gold schemas. Bronze and silver schemas are internal to the domain and may only be read by the Hub team for audit purposes.

-The Hub team maintains a shared platform catalog containing: a governance schema (data contracts, policy definitions, audit logs) and a discovery schema (a curated cross-domain data product registry). Cross-domain joined datasets are the responsibility of the consuming domain, not the Hub — the Hub does not produce analytical data products.

-Domain-to-domain data access is handled via Unity Catalog grants — a consuming domain requests read access to a producing domain's gold schema through the Hub team, which acts as access broker. Delta Sharing is the preferred mechanism for any data that must be shared outside the Databricks workspace (e.g. with external collaborators or CROs). Direct cross-catalog queries within the workspace are permitted for gold layer only. 

- To maintain an overview of accesses, access is granted only to groups (discovery-read, discovery-write, ...). The domain data product owners are responsible for making sure that people within their domain are part of the proper group.

- This is the catalog topology in practice:
platform/          ← Hub
  governance/
  discovery/
discovery/         ← Domain
  bronze/
  silver/
  gold/
translational_science/
  ...



## Status
Accepted.

## Consequences
The Hub/Spoke model trades domain agility for platform consistency. Concretely: domain teams cannot unilaterally introduce new ingestion patterns, compute types, or storage locations — all changes require Hub-provided templates or Hub approval. This slows individual domain velocity but prevents the platform from fragmenting into incompatible silos as the organization scales.

The medallion layer standard and Unity Catalog boundary definitions made here will constrain every pipeline built on this platform. If a domain's data does not fit cleanly into bronze/silver/gold (e.g. streaming sensor data from lab equipment), this architecture will require revisiting before that domain can onboard.

The annual roadmap cadence is a starting point for a small organization. It should be reviewed once the platform serves more than two active domains, as yearly cycles may be too slow to respond to domain needs during a period of rapid scaling.
